### §01 PROFILE

Senior commerce leader sitting between brand custody and country execution. Owns regional GMV growth and the digital/social commerce vendor strategy — typically the Ecomobi sponsor and champion at MNC FMCG accounts.

Typical background: 10-15 years of experience. Career path: brand management (4-6 years) → digital marketing manager → regional digital lead → regional commerce director. Many came up through Unilever, P&G, L'Oréal, or Reckitt management trainee programs. Some increasingly come from agency/tech side (ex-GroupM, ex-Lazada/Shopee) for data fluency.

Day-to-day: splits time between regional leadership meetings (performance reviews, planning), vendor governance (monthly/quarterly business reviews), and market-specific escalations where local execution needs regional backing. Spends disproportionate time trying to reconcile measurement frameworks from different vendors.

### §02 SNAPSHOT

**Public goals**:
- Achieve regional GMV growth target (typically 30-50% YoY)
- Reduce CAC across digital commerce channels
- Demonstrate incrementality of social commerce vs traditional channels
- Vendor consolidation: fewer, deeper relationships rather than fragmented stack
- Build internal capability — talent, tooling, measurement framework

**Private goals**:
- Be the person who "cracked social commerce in SEA" internally — career-defining narrative
- Avoid being blamed if a major bet doesn't work — needs defensible decisions
- Get a regional case study featured at global town hall
- Develop reputation as strategic operator, not just a vendor manager

**Typical pressures**:
- GMV target set 30-50% above prior year with flat or declining headcount budget
- Regional HQ demanding consistency across markets; local teams demanding flexibility
- Finance teams asking sharper questions about attribution and incrementality
- Procurement adding 2-4 weeks to every contract cycle
- Platform algorithm changes disrupting established creator strategies mid-campaign

**Decision-making style**: Data-driven with a strong relationship overlay. Will not commit without numbers, but numbers alone don't decide — needs trust in the operator behind the numbers. Consensus-seeking at regional level before deciding. Comfortable saying no quickly to obvious misfits; slow to say yes.

### §03 CARES ABOUT

1. GMV target achievement — it's the core performance metric
2. Incrementality proof — being able to demonstrate social commerce drives net new revenue
3. Vendor consolidation — reducing the coordination overhead that consumes team capacity
4. Peer credibility — being seen by other regional directors as someone who made smart bets
5. Attribution quality — the ability to show finance a clean picture of what drove what
6. Execution reliability — vendors who do what they say at the scale they promised
7. Regional/HQ alignment — getting country teams and global leadership both on board

### §04 PRESSURES

**From above**: GMV targets growing 30-50% YoY. Board expects social commerce to drive incremental growth, not cannibalize. Attribution credibility required for next budget cycle. HQ demands regional consistency.

**From below**: Country teams resist vendor mandates from regional level. Internal execution teams stretched. Data from country teams often doesn't reconcile to regional view.

**From the market**: Competitors moving faster. TikTok Shop algorithm changes disrupting playbooks mid-flight. Consumer trust shifting rapidly toward creator-mediated commerce.

### §05 DECISION MAKING

Needs multiple rounds of evidence: peer reference first, then case study economics, then measurement framework review, then often a studio visit or live observation. Won't commit without all four.

Consults: their direct team (brand managers, commerce ops), country GM for local viability signal, and informal peer network (other regional directors at non-competing MNCs — highest trust source).

Approval threshold: Typically can approve commitments up to $300-500K independently; larger contracts require regional GM or CMO sign-off plus procurement process.

Accelerators: warm intro from peer, verified case study at comparable scale, studio visit, measured and honest discussion of limitations.

Decelerators: over-promising, generic deck with no specifics, technology-first pitches, anything that implies their current decisions were wrong.

### §06 COMMUNICATION PREFERENCES

- **Format**: Executive summary first (lead with conclusion), then supporting detail. Deck for first formal meeting; data/case study file for follow-up. Never a narrative-only document without supporting numbers.
- **Cadence**: Bi-weekly to monthly during evaluation. Monthly business reviews once contracted. Weekly status emails during peak campaigns.
- **Tone**: Professional but not stiff. Direct. Specific over generic — names, numbers, dates over adjectives.
- **Meeting style**: Prepared agenda 24+ hours in advance. Comfortable with 30-min dense meetings. Impatient with 60-min meetings that could be 30.
- **What earns their time**: A specific, relevant insight (not a generic trend piece). A peer reference they didn't already know about. A concrete question with a yes/no answer they can act on.

### §07 PITCH PATTERNS

**What gets their attention**: Peer reference from another MNC at comparable scale within the first 90 seconds. Specific GMV outcome with attribution methodology explained (not just a headline number).

**What lands**: Case studies with full economics. Studio visit. Honest discussion of where you haven't worked — sophistication signal.

**What doesn't land**: Creator network size as a top-line stat. Generic "we work with major brands" name-drops. Aspirational decks heavy on platform trends — they read this research already.

**What NOT to say**: "We noticed your current vendors are fragmented" — true but condescending. Anything leading with technology before establishing execution credibility. "We're the only ones who can do this" — they've heard it from every vendor.

### §08 COMMON OBJECTIONS

**Objection**: We already have 4-5 vendors covering this.
**Response**: Frame as consolidation, not addition. Map current vendor outputs to the integrated stack — the conversation becomes "replace 2-3 of these" not "add another contract."

**Objection**: How do we know your creator network is real?
**Response**: Offer studio visit. Show real-time operations. Reference L'Oréal Tet 2024 at operational scale (2,000+ sessions). Network depth is demonstrated, not described.

**Objection**: We can't tell if this is incremental or channel cannibalization.
**Response**: Lead with attribution methodology — pre-period vs activation period comparison, holdout cells where feasible. Acknowledge attribution is genuinely hard; show rigor.

**Objection**: We need multi-market support; VN-only isn't enough.
**Response**: Honest answer: VN is the depth market; SEA expansion is in progress. Propose VN as proof-of-concept with explicit expansion roadmap.

**Objection**: Our procurement process will take months.
**Response**: Validate the timeline (don't fight it). Ask to be introduced to procurement early. Provide a term sheet that anticipates their standard requirements.

### §09 ROLE IN DEALS

Champion and economic buyer combined (when within their approval threshold). Primary sponsor who brings the deal to procurement and country GM. Needs to feel personally confident before going to bat internally.

Enters the process at Meeting 1. Must be re-engaged at every gate — they don't delegate final recommendation. Lose them by: surprising them at a senior meeting with information they didn't have, over-promising in writing before the contract is signed, or failing the studio visit / live ops credibility check.

### §10 ICP VARIATIONS

In **MNC/Global FMCG**: This persona is the primary champion and often co-buyer. Highest complexity, longest cycles, highest ACV.

In **Regional D2C Beauty**: Lighter version — may be a single "Head of E-commerce" with similar motivations but more hands-on and shorter cycle. Less procurement friction.

In **Vietnamese Domestic CPG**: Role often doesn't exist as a dedicated regional function — decision sits with Country Marketing Director or CEO. Different buyer dynamics entirely.

At early-stage social commerce maturity: More skeptical, wants a smaller pilot to test before committing. Frame as "prove it" engagement.

At advanced maturity: Already has an internal playbook; looking for operational lift and creator depth, not strategic education.
